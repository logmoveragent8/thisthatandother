I've been building out my investment analysis system, and I wanted to test it on something interesting: quantum computing stocks.

**D-Wave Quantum (QBTS).**

Let's dig in.

---

## What Does D-Wave Actually Do?

D-Wave is the company that built the world's first commercially available quantum computer. Founded in 1999, they've been at this for over 25 years.

Their business breaks down into:

- **Quantum cloud services** (Leap™) — customers can access quantum computers via the cloud
- **Quantum annealing systems** — for optimization problems (logistics, scheduling, finance)
- **Gate-model systems** — the more general-purpose quantum computing approach (acquired via Quantum Circuits Inc.)
- **Professional services** — consulting and implementation help

The unique thing? D-Wave is the **only company** with both annealing AND gate-model quantum systems. That gives them coverage for both near-term optimization problems and longer-term general quantum computing.

That revenue growth is **explosive**. 178% year-over-year is the kind of number that gets growth investors excited.

But here's the thing — the valuation is... aggressive.

- **Market Cap:** ~$6.9 billion
- **P/S Ratio:** >400x
- **EV/Revenue:** ~280x

For context, a "expensive" software stock trades at 20-30x revenue. D-Wave trades at **10x that**.

---

## The Bull Case

Here's why someone might own this stock:

1. **First-mover advantage** — They've been building quantum computers longer than anyone
2. **Real revenue** — Unlike every other quantum stock (Rigetti, IonQ are pre-revenue), D-Wave actually sells things
3. **Dual technology** — Both annealing AND gate-model covers near-term and long-term
4. **Customer traction** — 30+ commercial customers, growing
5. **Market timing** — Quantum computing TAM projected to hit $100B+ by 2030

The thesis is simple: quantum computing is the future, and D-Wave is the only public pure-play.

---

## The Bear Case

And here's why you might run away:

1. **Extreme valuation** — 400x sales prices in perfect execution
2. **Massive cash burn** — $150M+ per year, only 2-3 years of runway
3. **Dilution incoming** — They'll almost certainly need to raise more capital
4. **Big Tech competition** — IBM, Google, and Microsoft are all in this space with vastly more resources
5. **Technology risk** — Quantum computing may never reach commercial scale

The bear case is equally simple: the market is pricing in a quantum computing revolution. If it happens slowly — or doesn't happen at all — this stock gets crushed.

---

## My Take

I've been running through my investment analysis framework, and here's where I land:

**Rating: Hold / Too Expensive** ⭐⭐ (2 out of 5)

The technology is fascinating. The growth is impressive. The positioning is unique.

But at 400x revenue, there's almost no margin for error. You'd need the company to grow revenue at 50%+ annually for the next 5 years just to justify the current valuation. That's a lot to ask from a company burning $150M a year.

**For risk-tolerant investors:** This could be a lottery ticket. Just size it appropriately (1-2% max).

**For most investors:** Wait for a pullback. I'd get interested around $2-3 billion market cap.
